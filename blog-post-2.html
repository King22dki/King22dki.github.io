Neural Coding and Information Theory: A Cross-Disciplinary Perspective

January 22, 2026 14 min read Neuroscience, AI, Information Theory
Abstract: The parallel development of biological neuroscience and artificial neural networks has revealed deep connections between how biological brains and artificial systems process information. This article explores these connections through the lens of information theory, examining how principles of efficient coding discovered in neuroscience can inform the design of more robust and interpretable artificial intelligence systems. We investigate sparse coding, predictive processing, and temporal dynamics as bridges between these two domains.

Introduction

When Warren McCulloch and Walter Pitts published their seminal paper on neural networks in 1943, they drew direct inspiration from biological neurons. Since then, artificial neural networks have evolved in ways that sometimes align with and sometimes diverge from our understanding of biological systems. Yet the fundamental question remains: what can we learn from three billion years of neural evolution that might improve our artificial systems?

Information theory, developed by Claude Shannon in the late 1940s, provides a mathematical framework for understanding communication and computation that is agnostic to whether the substrate is biological or silicon. By examining how biological neural systems encode and process information through this lens, we can extract principles that transcend the specific implementation details. This cross-pollination between neuroscience and AI has already yielded insights into attention mechanisms, recurrent processing, and hierarchical representations. The next frontier lies in understanding temporal dynamics, energy efficiency, and robustness—areas where biology still dramatically outperforms artificial systems.

The Efficient Coding Hypothesis

Theoretical Foundations

In 1961, Horace Barlow proposed what would become known as the efficient coding hypothesis: neural systems have evolved to represent sensory information as efficiently as possible, given the constraints of noise, energy consumption, and limited communication capacity. This hypothesis provides a normative framework for understanding why neural systems exhibit certain properties.

From an information-theoretic perspective, efficiency means maximizing the mutual information between stimuli and neural responses while minimizing redundancy. Consider the optic nerve, which must transmit visual information from approximately 100 million photoreceptors in the retina to the brain using only about 1 million nerve fibers. This dramatic dimensionality reduction necessitates sophisticated encoding strategies.

The retina accomplishes this through several mechanisms: center-surround receptive fields that highlight edges and changes rather than uniform regions; adaptation that adjusts sensitivity based on the statistics of recent stimuli; and temporal decorrelation that emphasizes temporal changes over static scenes. Each of these mechanisms can be understood as reducing redundancy and maximizing information transmission subject to the bandwidth constraint of the optic nerve.

Sparse Coding in Biological and Artificial Systems

One of the most influential ideas to emerge from the efficient coding framework is sparse coding. In the visual cortex, particularly in area V1, neurons respond selectively to specific features—particular orientations, spatial frequencies, and locations. At any given moment, only a small fraction of neurons are highly active. This sparse activation pattern has several advantages: metabolic efficiency (since action potentials are energetically expensive), increased representational capacity, and enhanced robustness to noise.

Bruno Olshausen and David Field demonstrated in 1996 that when artificial neural networks are trained to represent natural images using a sparse coding objective, they develop receptive fields remarkably similar to those observed in V1 neurons. This convergent evolution suggests that sparsity is not an arbitrary design choice but an optimal solution to the problem of representing natural stimuli efficiently.

In artificial neural networks, sparse coding has been implemented through various mechanisms: L1 regularization encourages weight sparsity; dropout induces activation sparsity during training; and k-sparse autoencoders explicitly constrain the number of active neurons. These techniques often improve generalization and interpretability, suggesting that lessons from biological sparsity translate effectively to artificial systems.

Predictive Coding and Hierarchical Processing

The Brain as a Prediction Machine

A paradigm shift in computational neuroscience came with the realization that the brain is not merely passively processing incoming sensory data but actively predicting it. The predictive coding framework, developed by Rajesh Rao, Dana Ballard, and others, posits that the brain maintains hierarchical generative models of the world and constantly generates predictions about incoming sensory input. Only prediction errors—differences between predicted and actual input—are propagated up the hierarchy.

This framework elegantly explains numerous phenomena: why we don't notice our own eye movements (the brain predicts the resulting visual changes); why unexpected stimuli evoke stronger neural responses; and how top-down processing influences perception. From an information-theoretic perspective, predictive coding is efficient because it only transmits surprising information, effectively implementing a form of differential coding that reduces redundancy.

Implications for Artificial Neural Networks

Predictive coding has profound implications for AI architecture design. Traditional feedforward neural networks process information in a single bottom-up pass. While backpropagation provides error signals during training, there are no error signals during inference. In contrast, predictive coding architectures maintain bidirectional connections, with top-down predictions and bottom-up error signals flowing simultaneously.

Several modern AI architectures can be viewed through the predictive coding lens. Variational autoencoders (VAEs) learn generative models that predict input data and minimize reconstruction error. Generative adversarial networks (GANs) pit a generator making predictions against a discriminator identifying prediction errors. Transformer models with masked language modeling are essentially learning to predict missing inputs.

More explicitly, predictive coding networks (PCNs) have been developed that closely follow the biological framework. These networks update their internal states through gradient descent on prediction error, rather than through backpropagation. Surprisingly, despite their biological motivation, PCNs have shown competitive performance on various tasks and exhibit interesting properties like continual learning without catastrophic forgetting—a significant advantage over standard deep learning approaches.

Temporal Dynamics and Neural Oscillations

Time as a Computational Resource

While most artificial neural networks treat time discretely (if at all), biological neural systems are fundamentally dynamical systems operating in continuous time. Neural oscillations—rhythmic patterns of activity at various frequencies—are ubiquitous in the brain and appear to play crucial computational roles.

Oscillations at different frequencies seem to serve different functions: gamma oscillations (30-100 Hz) are associated with local processing and attention; beta oscillations (13-30 Hz) with top-down processing and maintaining the status quo; theta oscillations (4-8 Hz) with memory and sequential processing; and slower oscillations with longer-range coordination. These multiple timescales allow the brain to process information hierarchically in time as well as in space.

From an information-theoretic perspective, temporal dynamics provide additional channels for information encoding. Phase-of-firing codes, where the timing of spikes relative to ongoing oscillations carries information beyond firing rates, can dramatically increase coding capacity. Synchrony and desynchrony patterns enable dynamic routing of information—determining which neurons communicate effectively with which others.

Spiking Neural Networks and Temporal Coding

Spiking neural networks (SNNs) attempt to capture biological temporal dynamics more faithfully than rate-based models. Rather than representing neural activity as continuous values, SNNs use discrete action potentials—spikes—as the fundamental computational unit. This enables several potentially powerful features: event-driven computation that could be far more energy-efficient than clock-driven processing; precise temporal coding that could increase information capacity; and natural integration with neuromorphic hardware.

The challenge lies in training SNNs effectively. Backpropagation, the workhorse of deep learning, is difficult to apply directly to spiking networks because spike timings are not differentiable. Various approaches have been developed: surrogate gradients that approximate spike derivatives; conversion from trained artificial neural networks to SNNs; and biological learning rules like spike-timing-dependent plasticity (STDP) that adjust synaptic weights based on precise spike timing.

Recent work has shown that SNNs can achieve competitive accuracy on computer vision tasks while using orders of magnitude less energy than conventional neural networks, especially when implemented on neuromorphic chips like Intel's Loihi or IBM's TrueNorth. This energy efficiency derives from event-driven computation—neurons only consume energy when they spike, unlike artificial neurons that perform computations at every time step.

Attention, Salience, and Resource Allocation

Biological Attention Mechanisms

The brain must process a massive influx of sensory information with limited computational resources. Attention mechanisms solve this problem by selectively enhancing processing of behaviorally relevant information while suppressing irrelevant distractors. Neuroscience distinguishes between bottom-up attention (driven by stimulus salience) and top-down attention (driven by goals and expectations).

Bottom-up attention can be understood information-theoretically as detecting surprising or informative stimuli—regions with high local information content. Top-down attention, conversely, reflects the optimization of information gathering based on task demands and expected value. These two forms of attention interact continuously, with top-down predictions modulating bottom-up processing and surprising stimuli capturing top-down attention.

Attention in Artificial Neural Networks

Attention mechanisms have revolutionized AI, particularly in natural language processing. The transformer architecture, introduced in 2017, relies entirely on attention mechanisms without recurrent or convolutional layers. Self-attention allows the network to dynamically determine which parts of the input are relevant to each other, implementing a form of learned, data-driven routing.

While transformer attention mechanisms were not explicitly inspired by neuroscience, the parallels are striking. The query-key-value formulation loosely resembles biological attention, where neural populations generate signals (queries) that select among possible inputs (keys) to determine what information to process (values). Multi-head attention, which allows different attention heads to specialize in different types of relationships, echoes the brain's multiple attentional systems operating at different scales and for different purposes.

However, important differences remain. Biological attention is energy-aware, preferentially allocating resources to high-value information. Transformer attention mechanisms, by contrast, compute attention over all possible pairings, resulting in quadratic computational complexity. Biological attention operates continuously in time, with attention shifts taking tens to hundreds of milliseconds. Developing more biologically realistic attention mechanisms that balance comprehensiveness with efficiency remains an open challenge.

Robustness, Noise, and Uncertainty

Biological Strategies for Robust Computation

Biological neural systems operate in noisy, uncertain environments with unreliable components—individual neurons are inherently stochastic, and synaptic transmission fails a significant fraction of the time. Yet the brain achieves remarkable computational reliability despite this unreliability at the component level. This robustness emerges through several mechanisms: redundancy through population coding; error correction through feedback loops; and graceful degradation where performance decreases smoothly rather than catastrophically with damage.

From an information-theoretic perspective, noise sets fundamental limits on information transmission and processing. Shannon's noisy channel coding theorem proves that reliable communication is possible even over noisy channels, provided the transmission rate stays below the channel capacity. Biological neural systems implement sophisticated error-correction strategies that approach these theoretical limits.

Uncertainty Quantification in AI

Modern AI systems often fail catastrophically when confronted with out-of-distribution inputs or adversarial perturbations. They typically provide predictions without calibrated confidence estimates, making them unreliable in safety-critical applications. Incorporating biological principles of uncertainty representation could address these limitations.

Bayesian approaches to neural networks provide a principled framework for uncertainty quantification by treating weights as probability distributions rather than point estimates. This allows networks to express uncertainty about their predictions. However, exact Bayesian inference is intractable for large networks, necessitating approximations like variational inference or Monte Carlo dropout.

Another biologically inspired approach is probabilistic population codes, where uncertainty is represented through the distributed activity of neural populations rather than through explicit probability computations. Recent work has shown that standard neural networks can implicitly represent uncertainty through their activation patterns, and that this uncertainty can be extracted and calibrated post-hoc.

Energy Efficiency and Computational Constraints

The Brain's Energy Budget

The human brain consumes approximately 20 watts of power—about the same as a dim light bulb—yet performs computations that require megawatts when implemented on conventional computers. This extraordinary energy efficiency arises from several factors: sparse activity patterns where most neurons are quiet most of the time; analog computation that avoids the overhead of binary digital logic; local computation that minimizes communication costs; and adaptive precision that allocates bits where they matter most.

Energy constraints shape neural coding strategies. Action potentials are expensive, consuming much of the brain's energy budget. This cost encourages sparse, efficient coding where neurons fire only when they can convey significant information. Synaptic transmission is cheaper but still costly, favoring localized processing over long-distance communication.

Toward Energy-Efficient AI

As AI models grow ever larger, energy consumption has become a critical concern both economically and environmentally. Training GPT-3 reportedly consumed approximately 1,287 MWh of electricity. Biological principles offer potential paths toward more efficient AI: event-driven computation that processes information only when it changes; mixed-precision computation that uses high precision only where necessary; and in-memory computing that reduces the energy cost of shuttling data between memory and processors.

Neuromorphic computing attempts to implement these principles in hardware. Spiking neural networks running on neuromorphic chips can achieve energy efficiencies approaching biological levels for certain tasks. However, programming these systems remains challenging, and they currently excel primarily at specialized tasks rather than the general-purpose computation at which traditional computers excel.

Future Directions and Open Questions

Bridging Theory and Practice

Despite decades of research, significant gaps remain between theoretical neuroscience and practical AI. Information-theoretic analyses often make simplifying assumptions—Gaussian noise, linear neurons, static stimuli—that don't fully capture biological or artificial neural networks' complexity. Bridging this gap requires developing more sophisticated theoretical tools and more biologically realistic models.

One promising direction is disentangled representations, where different dimensions of neural activity encode independent factors of variation in the world. Neuroscience suggests the brain learns such disentangled representations, and recent AI research has shown they improve generalization and interpretability. However, we still lack a complete information-theoretic understanding of what makes representations disentangled and how to learn them efficiently.

Learning Rules and Credit Assignment

Backpropagation, while enormously successful in AI, is biologically implausible. It requires symmetric forward and backward weights, non-local error signals, and separate training and inference phases—none of which align with neural biology. Developing learning algorithms that are both biologically plausible and computationally effective remains a major challenge.

Recent proposals like feedback alignment, target propagation, and equilibrium propagation offer more biologically realistic alternatives. Predictive coding networks learn through local learning rules without explicit backpropagation. Whether these approaches can scale to the complexity of state-of-the-art AI systems while maintaining biological plausibility remains an open question.

Conclusion

Information theory provides a unifying language for understanding computation in both biological and artificial neural systems. By examining how brains encode and process information efficiently, we gain insights that can improve AI systems' robustness, efficiency, and interpretability. Sparse coding, predictive processing, temporal dynamics, and energy-efficient computation are not merely biological curiosities but principled solutions to fundamental computational challenges.

As AI systems become more complex and are deployed in increasingly critical applications, the need for systems that are robust, interpretable, and efficient grows ever more pressing. Biological neural systems, refined through millions of years of evolution, offer existence proofs that such systems are possible and hints at how to build them. The cross-pollination between neuroscience and AI is not merely academic—it represents a path toward artificial intelligence that is not only powerful but also reliable, efficient, and aligned with human values.

The journey from biological inspiration to practical AI is neither straightforward nor complete. Many biological mechanisms remain poorly understood, and not all biological solutions translate directly to artificial systems. Yet by viewing both biological and artificial neural systems through the lens of information theory, we can identify deep principles that transcend implementation details and guide the development of the next generation of AI systems.
