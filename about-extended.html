Who I am

First and foremost, curious

My journey through chemical engineering, teaching, and AI research has never followed a predetermined path. Instead, it has been guided by a quiet but persistent desire to understand how things work...from the molecular interactions within chemical systems, to the flow of information across neural networks, from abstract mathematical structures to the deeper question of consciousness itself.

It is perhaps at the intersections of these fields that the most meaningful insights begin to emerge. As an autodidact, I have found that knowledge rarely exists in isolation. Mathematics offers a language through which ideas can be expressed, engineering grounds those ideas within reality, neuroscience reveals how nature has already approached similar problems, and philosophy invites us to question whether we are even asking the right things. In moving between these domains, I do not see separation, but continuity...a space where connections begin to form, and understanding slowly unfolds.

"The important thing is not to stop questioning. Curiosity has its own reason for existing."

— Albert Einstein

Interests

Intellectual pursuits

My work spans multiple disciplines, though I do not treat them as separate. I am interested in the underlying structures that govern systems...how information flows, how patterns emerge, and how understanding itself is formed. Each field offers a lens, but it is in their intersection that the most meaningful insights arise.

Pedagogy sits at the centre of this. Teaching, to me, is the act of shaping how others come to think. It is where abstraction must become accessible, where ideas are structured, challenged, and internalised. It is not simply the transfer of knowledge, but the cultivation of understanding and agency.

I move between abstraction and application...from probability theory and higher-dimensional spaces, to neural systems, material research, and the philosophical limits of knowledge. These are not isolated pursuits, but part of a broader attempt to understand intelligence, reality, and how that understanding can be meaningfully shared.

Journey

Anything but linear

I began with chemical engineering, drawn to the challenge of designing processes that transform matter at scale. My undergraduate and master's work at London South Bank University and Imperial College London provided rigorous technical training, but also revealed gaps — particularly around uncertainty, variability, and the limitations of deterministic models.

This led me to probability theory and statistics, initially as tools for engineering but increasingly as fascinating subjects in their own right. How do we reason under uncertainty? How does randomness give rise to predictable patterns? These questions connect engineering, mathematics, and AI in ways I keep finding new angles on.

Teaching became another dimension of my journey. Working with students — from those struggling with basic concepts to those pushing at the frontiers — taught me as much as formal education ever did. It forced me to clarify my own understanding and to appreciate that intelligence manifests in many forms.

Co-founding Queryous represented a synthesis: applying AI research to educational challenges, making powerful technologies accessible to those who need them most, and working collaboratively on problems that matter. It's an ongoing experiment in translating ideas into impact.

Philosophy

How I approach research

Depth Over Breadth — But Both Matter

While I explore many fields, I believe in going deep enough to actually contribute rather than skimming surfaces. It's better to genuinely understand one difficult thing than to superficially know ten.

Rigour With Humility

Mathematical and scientific rigour matter — sloppy thinking leads to wrong conclusions. But so does intellectual humility. Our current theories are incomplete approximations, not final truths. The most interesting questions are often those we don't yet know how to formalise.

Theory Grounded in Reality

Abstract theory is beautiful, but it should ultimately connect to observable phenomena and practical problems. The best theories explain reality; the best applications leverage deep theory.

Interdisciplinary Synthesis

Many important problems require integrating insights from multiple fields. Chemical reactor optimisation might benefit from AI; understanding neural networks might benefit from neuroscience; both benefit from probability theory and information theory.

Life

Beyond the desk

Physical activities like bouldering and Brazilian Jiu Jitsu provide different modes of problem-solving — reading rock faces or opponents, making split-second decisions under pressure, learning through direct physical feedback. These aren't merely recreation but alternative forms of intelligence and learning that feed back into how I think.

Reading broadly — from technical papers to literature to philosophy — keeps my thinking fresh and prevents narrow specialisation. Attending seminars on diverse topics (philosophy of mind one week, quantum computing the next, political economy after that) exposes me to different ways of thinking and argumentation.

Gaming and drawing, though "hobbies," are also creative outlets and sources of inspiration. Games involve complex systems with emergent behaviour; drawing requires translating three-dimensional space to two dimensions, engaging different cognitive faculties than mathematical or verbal reasoning.

Values

What I stand for

Sustainability

As a chemical engineer, I'm acutely aware of humanity's impact on planetary systems. Developing sustainable technologies and processes isn't optional — it's existentially necessary.

Ethics & Responsibility

Technology is never neutral. AI systems can amplify biases, deepen inequalities, or concentrate power. As researchers and developers, we have ethical obligations to consider impacts and advocate for responsible deployment.

Accessibility & Democratisation

Knowledge and powerful tools shouldn't be the exclusive domain of elites. Making AI and advanced education accessible to smaller institutions, developing countries, and underserved communities is both ethically right and practically wise.

Intellectual Honesty

It's better to say "I don't know" than to pretend certainty. It's better to acknowledge the limitations of one's methods than to overstate conclusions. Science progresses through error correction, not through defending positions.

Looking forward

Where next?

The honest answer is: I don't know exactly, and I'm okay with that. Rigid plans often miss unexpected opportunities. But some directions feel genuinely generative right now: understanding how neural networks represent and process information; applying stochastic processes to real-world systems; using AI thoughtfully to make education more effective and accessible; and building bridges between fields that don't usually talk to each other.

Ultimately, I hope to contribute to understanding — of intelligent systems, of complex processes, of how we learn and know. If along the way I can help others learn, solve practical problems, and make technologies more beneficial and accessible, even better.
